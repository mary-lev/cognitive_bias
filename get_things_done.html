Sunk cost fallacy

The Sunk Cost Bias, also known as the Sunk Cost Fallacy, is a cognitive bias that influences decision-making processes. It occurs when a person continues a behavior or endeavor as a result of previously invested resources (time, money, or effort), even if it's no longer beneficial to do so. This bias leads to irrational decision-making, as future decisions should ideally be based on the potential future benefits, not past sunk costs.

The bias is called "sunk" because these costs cannot be recovered or reversedâ€”they are irretrievably "sunk" into the endeavor. Despite this, individuals often let sunk costs influence their decisions, leading to suboptimal outcomes.

For instance, a person might continue to dine at an unpleasant restaurant because they've purchased a yearly membership, even though they're not enjoying their meals there. This decision to continue is driven by the sunk cost (the cost of the membership), rather than a rational evaluation of the quality of the food or dining experience.

  1. Watching a Bad Movie: You start watching a movie that turns out to be terrible, but you decide to watch it to the end because you've already spent an hour on it.
  2. Eating Beyond Fullness: You're full but there's still food on your plate. You decide to finish the food because you paid for it, even though you're already uncomfortably full.
  3. Staying in a Bad Relationship: A person might stay in an unhealthy or unfulfilling relationship because they've already invested a lot of time and emotion into it.
  4. Holding onto Bad Investments: An investor continues to hold onto poorly performing stocks because they've already lost a significant amount of money on them, hoping they will bounce back.
  5. Reading a Boring Book: You're halfway through a book that you find uninteresting, but you decide to finish reading it because you've already spent a lot of time on it.
  6. Continuing a Project: A business continues to fund a failing project because they've already invested a lot of resources into it, despite clear indications that the project is unlikely to generate a positive return.
  7. Unfinished Degree: A student who no longer has interest or sees value in their degree might continue their studies because they have already spent years and a lot of money on tuition.
  8. Prolonged Use of a Broken Device: You continue to use an old, malfunctioning laptop because you paid a lot for it, even though getting a new one would improve your productivity.
  9. Attending Unenjoyable Events: You go to a concert, even though you no longer like the band, because you had already purchased the tickets.
  10. Living in an Unsatisfactory Location: You continue living in a neighborhood or city that you don't like because you bought a house there, despite the fact that moving would improve your happiness and quality of life.
