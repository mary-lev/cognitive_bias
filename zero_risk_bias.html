Zero-Risk Bias

Zero-risk bias is a tendency to prefer the complete elimination of risk in a sub-part over alternatives with greater overall risk reduction. It often manifests in cases where decision makers address problems concerning health, safety, and the environment. Its effect on decision making has been observed in surveys presenting hypothetical scenarios

ChatGPT's Explanation

Zero-risk bias is a cognitive bias wherein people prefer the complete elimination of a risk in one area to a greater reduction in risk across multiple areas, even if the latter option results in a greater overall decrease in risk. Essentially, this bias highlights a preference for certainty and a complete absence of risk in specific scenarios, even if this comes at the cost of higher overall risk.

This bias can be seen in decision-making scenarios where individuals or groups prioritize solutions that completely eliminate a small, isolated risk rather than opting for solutions that reduce greater overall risks but do not completely eliminate any single risk. This can lead to inefficient allocation of resources, such as time, money, and effort, towards solutions that feel more secure because they remove uncertainty in a specific area, even though they might not be the most effective or rational use of those resources.

Examples of usage

  • Public Health Policies:
    • Scenario: During a flu epidemic, a city government has the option to either vaccinate 100% of the population against the flu (eliminating the risk of flu completely in the population) or use the same funds to vaccinate against both the flu and another more deadly disease, such as pneumonia, significantly reducing but not eliminating the risk of both diseases.
    • Zero-Risk Bias: The government chooses to vaccinate only against the flu to completely eliminate that risk, despite the option to achieve a greater overall health benefit by partially reducing the risk of two diseases.
  • Environmental Policy:
    • Scenario: A community can allocate a budget to entirely clean up a small, local contaminated lake or use the funds to implement larger-scale reductions in pollution across several lakes that do not completely cleanse any single lake but significantly improve the water quality in all.
    • Zero-Risk Bias: The community chooses to clean up the small lake completely, achieving zero risk of contamination there, while neglecting broader, more impactful environmental improvements.
  • Business Strategy:
    • Scenario: A company must decide between investing in a security system that completely eliminates data breaches in one small department or a more comprehensive IT upgrade that significantly reduces the risk of data breaches company-wide but does not eliminate it in any area.
    • Zero-Risk Bias: The company opts for the security system for the one department, prioritizing the elimination of all risk in that area, despite a larger potential risk remaining in other parts of the company.
  • Personal Health Choices:
    • Scenario: An individual must decide between taking a medication that completely eliminates the risk of a non-life-threatening but chronic condition, or a different medication that significantly reduces the risk of several more serious conditions but does not completely eliminate any of them.
    • Zero-Risk Bias: The individual chooses the medication that eliminates the risk of the chronic condition, ignoring the potentially more significant health benefits of reducing multiple higher risks.

These examples illustrate how zero-risk bias can lead to choices that may not be optimal when viewed from a broader perspective, focusing instead on eliminating uncertainty in smaller, more specific contexts.

Creating an Ontology

Classes
  • DecisionContext:
    • Description: Represents the environment or situation in which decisions are made.
    • Attributes: contextID, description, stakeholders
  • Risk:
    • Description: Represents potential negative outcomes that decision-makers aim to mitigate.
    • Attributes: riskID, description, severity, probability
  • Intervention:
    • Description: Actions or strategies implemented to mitigate risks.
    • Attributes: interventionID, description, cost, effectiveness
  • Outcome:
    • Description: Represents the results of choosing a particular intervention.
    • Attributes: outcomeID, description, overallRiskReduction
  • DecisionMaker:
    • Description: Individuals or groups making decisions.
    • Attributes: decisionMakerID, name, role, riskPreference
    • Subclasses:
      • BiasedAgent
        • Description: Decision-makers who are influenced by zero-risk bias, favoring interventions that eliminate risks completely in specific areas.
        • Additional Attributes: preferredRiskToEliminate (linked to specific Risk instances)
      • RationalAgent
        • Description: Decision-makers who evaluate risks and interventions more holistically, aiming for the greatest overall risk reduction.
        • Additional Attributes: riskAssessmentMethod (e.g., "Expected Value", "Cost-Benefit Analysis")
    Classes Properties
    • hasRisk:(DecisionContext -> Risk)
      • Description: Links a decision context to the risks considered within it.
    • considersIntervention: (DecisionContext -> Intervention)
      • Description: Links a decision context to potential interventions being considered.
    • leadsToOutcome: (Intervention -> Outcome)
      • Description: Describes the outcome resulting from an intervention.
    • hasPreference: (DecisionMaker -> Risk)
      • Description: Links a decision maker to a specific risk they are particularly focused on mitigating.
    • makesDecision: (DecisionMaker -> Intervention)
      • Description: Indicates which intervention is chosen by the decision maker.
    Relationships
    • influences: (DecisionMaker -> DecisionContext)
      • Description: Describes how a decision maker influences or controls the decision-making environment.
    • impacts: (Risk -> Outcome)
      • Description: Indicates how a specific risk impacts the outcome of a decision context when a particular intervention is or is not implemented.
    • prioritizes: (DecisionMaker -> Intervention)
      • Description: Indicates a preference for interventions that completely mitigate a risk, reflecting zero-risk bias.
    Attributes
    • Risk:
      • severity (numerical): Indicates how severe the risk is considered.
      • probability (numerical): Represents the likelihood of the risk occurring.
    • Intervention:
      • cost (numerical): The resources (e.g., money, time) required to implement the intervention.
      • effectiveness (numerical): A measure of how effective the intervention is in reducing the risk.
    • Outcome:
      • overallRiskReduction (numerical): Measures the total reduction in risk as a result of the intervention across all considered risks.
    • DecisionMaker:
      • riskPreference (enumeration): Could include preferences like "Zero-Risk", "Risk-Averse", "Risk-Neutral", "Risk-Tolerant".
