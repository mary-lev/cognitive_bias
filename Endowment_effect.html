Endowment effect

Examples of usage

The Endowment Effect is a cognitive bias that causes people to ascribe more value to things merely because they own them. This can lead to irrational decision-making, such as holding onto items longer than beneficial or pricing them higher than market value when selling. Here are few examples:

  • Collectibles and Personal Items: Individuals might value their own collections or personal items (like books, watches, or jewelry) much higher than an impartial market value, making it hard to sell these items at their expected prices.
  • Stock Market Investments: Investors might hold onto stocks or other securities longer than advisable because they overvalue their current portfolio due to personal ownership, even in the face of logical indicators suggesting they should sell.
  • Product Purchases: Once a consumer purchases an item, they often value it more than they did prior to purchase, which can affect their satisfaction with the product and their decisions regarding future purchases or exchanges.
