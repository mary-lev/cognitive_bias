Project
Overview

This website presents our group project for the Knowledge Representation and Knowledge Extraction course taught by Professor Aldo Gangemi at the University of Bologna. Here we record our trivial attempts to model the fascinating phenomena of our brain and cognitive processes, focusing on human cognitive biases, and try to represent them in a formal way using Linked Open Data techniques.

What did we do

Our works mainly contribute to revealing a tiny part of the Cognitive Biases Codex, which is Wikipedia's complete (as of 2016) list of cognitive biases, arranged and designed by John Manoogian III. Our topics fall into the context of when we “need to act fast”, and these cognitive biases cunningly happen to our brains:

  • To get things done, we tend to complete things we've invested time and energy in;
  • We favor simple-looking options and complete information over complex, ambiguous options.

Domain

The domain covered by this ontology should encompass psychology, behavioral economics, decision-making processes, and related fields.

Application

The ontology should be used for understanding, categorizing, and analyzing cognitive biases to improve decision-making, enhance critical thinking, and develop strategies to mitigate their impact.

Since we did not have a specific application for our project, we decided to define it ourselves, and it seems that the most obvious goal of the cognitive bias ontology is to decide whether or not a person tends to have a bias in his or her behavior or plans or decisions. So we need to describe the possible situations and the particular contexts in which the person may be affected by the biases.

The knowledge represented in the ontology should answer questions related to the identification of specific cognitive biases, their underlying mechanisms, their effects on decision-making, their potential causes, and strategies to counteract or minimize their influence.

Competence Questions

  1. What are the common cognitive biases observed in decision-making processes?
  2. How do cognitive biases influence human behavior in various contexts?
  3. What are the cognitive mechanisms underlying specific biases?
  4. How can cognitive biases be identified and measured?
  5. What strategies can be employed to mitigate the effects of cognitive biases?
  6. How do individual differences contribute to susceptibility to certain biases?
  7. What are the real-world consequences of unchecked cognitive biases?
  8. How do cultural factors influence the manifestation of cognitive biases?

Existed ontologies to reuse

By leveraging the existing ontologies, developers can benefit from established frameworks and vocabularies, potentially reducing redundancy and ensuring interoperability with other knowledge resources. Therefore, to reuse some already well-established ontology frameworks, we get inspired by the following frameworks and core ontologies designed specifically to describe and model decision-making:

  • A Core Ontology on Decision Making
  • The Decision Ontology (DO)
  • Ontology of Value, Risk and Cognitive Biases for an IT Portfolio Decision Making

Besides these frameworks, we also tried to integrate some ontologies and vocabularies for general use (eg. foaf, DCcores, schemas…) into our ontologies.

Workflow

To finish our project fast, ironically, we attempted to work with the following methods:

  • Split our work into relatively inorganic fragments, where each member of our group takes charge of 7-8 sub-biases under the two main branches;
  • Play with GPT-4 a lot, who is extremely quick at OWL coding, and is always happy with giving us insightful suggestions;
  • Imagine our cognitive biases in the context of human decision-making processes, from which research area we can get abundant literature and even well-designed ontologies waiting to be reused;
  • Model our biases in a binary forked road, based on the Homo economicus hypothesis, we assume that humans are either rational and capable of maximizing their self-interests with modern economic analysis skills, or irrational when they are victims of the cognitive biases, and henceforth fail to make self-interested decisions;
  • Manifest the existence of these cognitive biases with our own actions!
