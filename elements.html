Conclusion

Our project aimed to formalize human cognitive biases using ontology modeling techniques. We developed an ontology that captures various aspects of cognitive biases, decision-making processes, and their implications across different domains. We leveraged existing frameworks and vocabularies to ensure interoperability and alignment with established standards. Our approach combined top-down and bottom-up strategies, allowing us to refine the ontology based on feedback, insights, and real-world validation.

*

By leveraging existing frameworks and vocabularies, such as the Decision Ontology and ontologies from the Dublin Core, FOAF, and Schema.org, we ensured interoperability and alignment with established standards. In addition, our iterative approach, combining top-down and bottom-up strategies, allowed us to iteratively refine the ontology based on feedback, insight, and real-world validation.

*

Through dialogue with ChatGPT-4, we have enriched our ontology with real-world examples and insights, ensuring that it reflects the complexity and nuance of human decision-making processes. While our project may only scratch the surface of the wide landscape of cognitive biases, it serves as a starting point for further research and development in this fascinating area.

Do we rely too much on ChatGPT？

Instead of using commonly recognized ontology editors like Protégé, we directly use GPT-4 to convert designed classes, properties, methods and relations from natural language descriptions into formal ontologies with the TTL syntax.

Do we rely on ChatGPT so much that we even handed over the whole coding process to it? The answer is unfortunately yes, this is absolutely not a standard method to design ontologies, whether we should embrace it or not is going to be a question open to doubting and debating.

However, the performance of GPT-4 in terms of OWL codings was beyond our expectations, and most generated OWL files can pass validation and visualization without further efforts and modifications, not to mention that GPT-4 can correct itself under our instructions. This quick approach largely increased our productivity, a large amount of errors possibly made by our group of OWL newbies were avoided with the help of LLM.

Tips and Tricks

During our conversation with ChatGPT it happens that sometimes it goes too deep into the given context and continues generating the content in the same scope. In such cases it does not make much sense to try to correct it and sometimes the better solution will be to start a new chat with an improved prompt.

Is there a way to combine the ontology of our biases into a single ontology?

Yes, we could do this, but the result will depend very much on the potential application of the ontology. Do we need to describe all possible situations in which the bias may affect our decisions? Or do we need to capture the broad categories and relationships between different biases? Depending on the specific requirements and scope of the application, we can design the combined ontology to accommodate different levels of granularity and detail.

Is there a way to combine the ontology of our biases into a single ontology?

Yes, we could do this, but the result will depend very much on the potential application of the ontology. Do we need to describe all possible situations in which the bias may affect our decisions? Or do we need to capture the broad categories and relationships between different biases? Depending on the specific requirements and scope of the application, we can design the combined ontology to accommodate different levels of granularity and detail.

One approach to combining multiple ontologies is to identify commonalities and overlaps between them and integrate them into a unified framework. This involves aligning the classes, properties, and relationships from each ontology and resolving any conflicts or inconsistencies. By mapping the concepts from different ontologies to a common domain model, we can create a more comprehensive and cohesive representation of cognitive biases.

Another approach is to modularize the ontology by using our existing ontologies as separate modules or namespaces for each bias or group of related biases. This allows for easier management, maintenance, and extension of the ontology while still allowing for interoperability and integration with other ontologies.

Ultimately, the design and structure of the combined ontology should be adapted to the specific needs and goals of the intended application to make sure that it is effective in representing the complexity of human cognition and decision making.
