Ambiguity Bias

Examples of usage

The Ambiguity Bias is a cognitive bias where individuals tend to favor known risks over unknown risks. This bias leads people to avoid options with unknown probabilities in favor of those with known probabilities, even if the expected outcomes are potentially more favorable in the ambiguous options. Here are few examples:

  • Investment Decisions: Investors might prefer investing in well-known stocks or bonds with a clear history of returns over new ventures or markets where the risks and potential returns are less well understood, even if the latter could lead to higher gains.
  • Healthcare Choices: Patients might opt for traditional treatment methods with well-documented outcomes rather than newer, potentially more effective treatments that lack extensive testing and results history.
  • Insurance Purchases: People are more likely to buy insurance for risks they understand and can quantify (like auto accidents) than for risks that are harder to quantify or are less familiar (such as certain types of natural disasters in their area).
