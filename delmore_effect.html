Delmore Effect

Examples of usage

Individuals focus on completing minor tasks or goals due to the immediate satisfaction and sense of achievement they provide, while neglecting more significant, challenging goals that could have a substantial impact on their happiness and life quality. Here are few examples:

  • Workplace Productivity: An employee spends their day clearing out their email inbox, organizing files, and attending to minor administrative tasks that offer a sense of completion. These tasks are straightforward and provide immediate satisfaction through the visual progress of an empty inbox or a tidy desk. However, this focus on minor accomplishments comes at the expense of working on a major project that requires deep focus and creative thinking. The project has the potential to significantly advance their career or contribute to the company's innovation, but it remains neglected due to its complexity and the longer timeframe required to see results.
  • Personal Fitness Goals: An individual sets a resolution to get in shape by joining a gym, buying workout clothes, and creating a detailed exercise schedule. Initially, they feel a sense of achievement from these preparatory actions, checking off each step as a move towards their fitness goals. However, they consistently choose to engage in easier, less impactful activities like short walks or light, sporadic workouts, avoiding the more challenging and time-consuming workouts that would lead to significant health improvements and weight loss. The immediate gratification and lesser effort of small tasks overshadow the commitment needed to achieve their substantial fitness goals.
  • Home Improvement Projects: A homeowner has a critical renovation project to enhance their home's value and livability, such as fixing a leaky roof or updating an outdated and inefficient heating system. Instead, they find themselves repeatedly distracted by smaller, more manageable tasks around the house, like repainting a room, rearranging furniture, or gardening. These tasks offer a quick sense of accomplishment and aesthetic improvements but do not address the more significant issues that could lead to severe consequences if left unattended. The homeowner prefers the simplicity and immediate rewards of minor projects over the daunting complexity and higher costs of essential renovations.

Creating an Ontology

Classes:
  • Agent (Person). Represents individuals subject.
    • hasName
    • hasMotivationLevel
    • hasGoalPriority
  • Task. Actions or activities an agent might consider.
    • hasDescription
    • hasComplexityLevel
    • hasCompletionTime
  • Goal. Represents more significant, impactful objectives.
    • hasLongTermImpact
    • hasRequiredEffort
Object Properties:
  • performsTask
    • Domain: Agent
    • Range: Task
    • Description: Links an agent to a task they consider or undertake.
  • makesDecision
    • Domain: Agent
    • Range: Decision
    • Description: Represents the cognitive process of an agent choosing between tasks and goals.
Axioms and Rules:
  • Rule Example: If an Agent makesDecision to perform a Task with hasComplexityLevel "Low" over a Goal with hasLongTermImpact "High", it indicates a manifestation of the Delmore effect.
